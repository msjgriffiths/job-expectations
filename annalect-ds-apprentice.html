Base Skills

The following is organized as a table. Each row is a bucket, and each cell is a collection of behaviors that related that to bucket. It some cases the bucket can be very general, and in other cases highly specific. This classification is not intended to be authoritative, and is subject to change (feel free to disagree or ask for classification).

Please note that no one can do all of these at the Advanced level. Rather, these are skills that an employee can choose to focus on.

For example, not everyone needs to be very good at presentation creation or delivery. In many cases it would not be effective to spend a lot of time improving presentation capability because the application is limited.

Additionally, some skills are more or less important. An inability to communicate effectively – in both comprehension and articulation – increases the amount of work that you, and the rest of the team, need to do. At best, this is inefficient.

Outcomes

The best way to approach a job is to understand that people care out outcomes, and not inputs. Frequently, the work each employee contributes affects only a small part of the outcome. It's easy to focus on your piece, and not the total outcome, since (a) that what you know and (b) that's what you can control. However, to be truly effective, it's important to examine the actual output and understand how the work you do contributes.

This perspective is important because it provides a framework in which you can evaluate changes in your own work. Is there something you can do that would increase the quality of the outcome, but that nobody has asked for? Does changing some way in which you deliver or perform the work affect how useful the work is? For example, delivering an analysis in Excel might be more helpful if people are likely to use the Excel document to investigate the analysis. But it might be less helpful if you know the people looking at the result won't have time to look around, or wouldn't understand what they were looking at if they did. In that case, it makes sense to deliver the results differently (perhaps as a document explaining the results and their interpretation, or as PowerPoint slides people can drop into a presentation with slide notes describing the slides) - even if they actually ask for an Excel document. You should "push back" and propose delivering differently. (Note: don't deliver something different without asking first. Your judgement may be wrong).

Teamwork

Teamwork is important because it can significantly increase the quality or quantity of outcomes. By "teamwork" people mean two things: alignment, and (lack of) friction. Alignment is the idea that everyone is working on the same thing, and trying to achieve the same goal. Even if any individual disagrees about the goal or how the team is doing it, they still work to the best of their ability to achieve that goal or ensure its success. Disagreement can – and should – occur, but that should not translate to sabotaging efforts by withdrawing contributions.

Friction has to do with how easily things can happen. Practically, at the individual level, the goal is to make each interaction you (the employee) has with any other member of the team as easy as possible. More formally, to reduce the transaction cost of interactions. The easier someone is to interact, the more interaction people will want to do, and the more interactions will take place: thus, the greater the value (ceteris paribus). Exceptions are where interactions ("transactions") do not add value, and their frequency "crowds out" valuable work.

General Business Skills

General skills apply to all office workers.

Skill Basic Basic level of competence. Intermediate Good competence – a few years of focus increasing that skill. Advanced An expert at this skill.

Comprehension – Verbal

How well the employee can understand verbal communication.
  • Employee can understand conversational English.
  • Employee does not interrupt, etc.
  • Employee asks questions to clarify.
  • Employee performs research on their own to verify their understanding.
  • Employee paraphrases key points to verify understanding.
  • Employee does not assume they know what the speaker meant, and act on that assumption. Verify understanding first.
  • Employee sends a summary email afterwards.
  • Employee employs active listening.
  • Employee highlights likely areas of misunderstanding and verifies multiple times.

Comprehension – Written

How well the employee can undestand written material.
  • Employee can understand written English.
  • Employee identifies language that is ambiguous or has multiple interpretations and requests clarification.
  • Employee can read complex information rapidly. For example, academic papers.
  • Employee paraphrases key points and responds with a summary of the critical information.
  • Employee can comfortably read pretty crazy stuff. Like this.

Articulation – Verbal

How well the employee can communicate their opinion verbally.
  • Employee can speak conversational English.
  • Employee uses short, clear sentences.
  • Employee structures their communication in a logical sequence. This may require drafting, on paper, a response – either in bullet points or script.
  • Employee avoids repeating information unnecessarily.
  • Employee can respond clearly and concisely rapidly (e.g. in a meeting, with little preparation)
  • Employee only communicates the necessary information for the next step: they do not include information that might be relevant to them but not to the audience.

Articulation – Written

How well the employee can articulate their point in writing.
  • Employee can write conversational English.
  • Employee employs short sentences. (this isn't 100% necessary: if you read the 6 grammar books I have you can get an exemption)
  • Employee uses paragraphs, bullet points, and tables to communicate information clearly.
  • Employee communications are appropriate to deliver to existing client.
  • Employee requests help drafting or editing emails / other documents.
  • Employee does not use ambiguous language.
  • Employee varies detail and style by recipient.
  • Employee can communicate well with anyone (e.g. very senior individuals at existing or prospective clients, etc).

Presentation - Construction

How well an employee can map out and create a presentation.
  • Employee can place text and images on a PowerPoint slide.
  • Employee can develop a logical structure to present their material.
  • Employee understands the distinction between "boardroom" (more text) and "ballroom" (more images) style presentations.
  • Employee understands how slide layout affects understanding.
  • Employee pays close attention to overall presentation narrative.

Presentations - Delivery

How well an employee can deliver a presentation.
  • Employee can read the content on their slides.
  • Employee is comfortable covering material on their slides.
  • Employee does not read the slide.
  • The employee is comfortable delivering to low-pressure audiences (team members, small groups)
  • The employee is comfortable delivering in high-pressure situations.
  • Employee practices the presentation multiple times before delivery. Bonus points for recording and reviewing.

Organization

How organized the employee is – files on their computer, emails, etc.
  • Employee keeps all emails and documents in a single folder (e.g. Inbox, Documents, Desktop)
  • Employee removes unncessaryor obsolete documents.
  • Employee retains all documents and information
  • Employee organizes emails and documents into logical groups (folders).
  • Employee uses a logical, consistent naming scheme for files and folders.
  • Employee documents naming scheme and folder structure.
  • Employee documents, for each folder, why the folder exists and what it relates to.

Note Taking

How completely the employee takes notes, how accurate they are, and how well they use the notes.
  • Employee takes notes on important topics.
  • Employee takes notes on everything.
  • Employee takes notes in digital format.
  • Employee takes notes on information that applies to other team members (but not necessarily them).
  • Employee distributes notes to other team members.
  • Employee organizes notes over time (ideally in some sensible system, like a wiki).

Information Availability*

The ability of a manager or other team member to find something out about work for which the employee is responsible
  • Employee provides information when requested.
  • Requires multiple follow ups to obtain all information.
  • Response may be delayed
  • Responds to requests for updates immediately.
  • Communicates all related information at once; never requires follow up for clarification / confirmation.
  • Makes all information available prior to a request (either "pull" – updating a shared document, or "push", e.g. regular, detailed update emails).
  • Anticipates need for information and highlights before request.
  • Anticipates need for unavailable information and works to obtain ahead of a request.

Value Delivery*

Understanding of the consequences of their work on other people in the organization and on the client, and knowledge of how other people use the work they produce.

  • The employee works to optimize their own work.
  • The employee works to understand how the work they deliver impacts the rest of the organization.
  • The employee suggests performing work differently to increase the total value added.
  • The employee proposes new work that can be used by team members.
  • The employee keeps track of the financial performance of both their division (e.g. Annalect) and the entire company (e.g. OMD + Omnicom).
  • The employee understands how their work affects the Profit & Loss statement (real or virtual) of the organization.
  • The employee focuses their own contributions on work that has the largest ROI
  • The employee identifies work their team members are doing that could be changed to have a higher ROI, and makes suggestions.

Learning Rate*

How fast the employee can learn things, and how much they learn.
  • Employee learns material when asked to.
  • Employee researches to learn what skills / knowledge gaps they have.
  • Employee pro-actively learns material to improve in their job.
  • Employee takes advantage of projects and tasks at work to develop or push their skills.
  • Employee has a scheduled learning plan mapped out.
  • Employee has defined career goals and a plan to learn what is required.
  • Employee works to create opportunities at work where they can build certain skills.

Independence*

The ability of the employee to act without explicit direction.
  • Employee performs work as directed.
  • Employee generates suggestions of work to do.
  • Employee proposes improved ways of doing work. Ideally using a decision briefing format.
  • Employee takes action independently on small tasks.
  • Employee creates (sufficiently) detailed plans for improvements.
  • Employee "lays the groundwork" for changes or work – that is, goes and gets everything ready, such that they only thing holding it back is getting "Yes" from their manager.
  • Employee is very diligent about reporting all work done to their manager.

Diminishing Returns

Understanding of the point of diminishing returns, and how that applies to their work.
  • Employee performs their work to the best of their ability.
  • Employee works to improve output to the point where they like it.
  • Employee understands the how any improvement or change made would affect team members who consume their work.
  • Employees understand the time value of money, and try to focus their efforts on work that has higher ROI.
  • Employee tries to focus their time on work with the highest benefit to the organization, given diminishing returns.
  • Employee actively discusses changes and improvements in terms of the time value of money.

Data Scientist

Skills that apply to a data scientist. The most important thing to realize as a data scientist is that you exist to help people make decisions. There are three parts of that:

  • Help: Helping is to focus on the other person and what they need. It's much less relevant what you can do (e.g. unsupervised clustering technique with clever variable transforms to improve outcomes) than how you can help (e.g. identifying and providing a few simple business rules to separate groups). If the end (business) user needs to explain the results of the work, and you deliver a complex result that requires another two weeks of work to interpret and understand the clusters, you haven't helped.
  • People: Different people interact with the world differently, and come from different levels of knowledge. As a data scientist, part of you job is to bridge the gap between their understanding and what you can contribute (i.e. it's not on them to communicate to you in a way you understand: that's your job).
  • Decisions: In most cases (in an agency) the goal is to make a decision. That might be more abstract — "What kind of people should we show ads to?" — or concrete, e.g. "Which of my ads had an impact on consumers?." You should focus on the decision that someone needs to make. Performing work in isolation of understanding that decision is unlikely to be useful.

All of this is complicated by how difficult it is to work in an organziation.

Considerations:

  • Most people are bad at explaining the question they want to answer.
  • Many people assume that statistics or machine learning is "magic"
  • People may not understand the different types of errors
  • The data source can have a tremendous impact

It may help to think of the job – that of a statistician or a data analyst – in terms of impact. What change can a data analyst have in an organization, in the best possible world? Ideally, a data analyst can extract:

  • All meaning from data: There may be information or patterns in the data that non-experts would overlook, not understand, or not think to look for. In a perfect world, an analyst would ensure that all possible meaning is extracted from a data set.
  • Avoid errors: People enjoy making decisions based on their personal opinions. A data analyst can ensure that decisions (a) are backed by data, (b) use statistics to reduce the chance of error, and (c) identify where data may be necessary to collect, now or in the future.

Neither of these are possible in practice, but it's possible to get closer.

Skill Basic Basic level of competence. Intermediate Good competence – a few years of focus increasing that skill. Advanced An expert at this skill.

Question Formulation

Can the analyst generate testable questions from business needs?
  • The analyst can generate simple questions.
  • Questions are focused on easily answering the problem posed to the analyst.
  • The question(s) generated may not address the full business need.
  • Questions directly address business need, even when business need is not stated directly or only communicated clumsily.
  • Question may approach the problem "sideways" – using the answer from an easier-to-answer question to provide an answer to the question posed.
  • Questions serve to clarify the actual business need, and/or reveal related or more important issues.
  • Questions may make the problem "disappear", revealing it as a pseudo-problem.
  • Questions are short, simple, and easy to communicate to team members or clients.

Techniques - Selection

What criteria does the analyst use to choose one technique over another?
  • Analyst uses techniques they are most comfortable with.
  • Analyst can provide explanation of why they chose one method over another.
  • Analyst can provide documentation of tests and interpretations explaining why one technique is more appropriate.
  • Analyst includes references to papers or textbooks.

Validity

To what extent does the analyst address potential validity issues?
  • Analyst perform analysis and basic checks (e.g. multiple comparison checks / corrections).
  • Analyst performs checks to verify that assumptions about data are held and enable use of a technique.
  • Analyst provides documentation of checks and references used in interpretation.
  • Analyst considers measurement error in data collection.
  • Analyst considers conceptual validity – does the data really enable investigation into the issue at hand?

Data Collection

How well can the analyst pull information they need or can employ for their analysis?
  • Data needs to be provided to the analyst in an easy-to-consume format (e.g. flat files).
  • Analyst can connect to API endpoints to pull data.
  • Analyst can use SQL to pull from databases.
  • Analyst can scrape content from semistructured sources (e.g. web).
  • Analyst can create large-scale scraping pipelines.
  • Analyst can perform complex ETL from multiple heterogenous sources.
  • Analyst can extract content from publication formats, e.g. PDF tables or scanned images (OCR)

Data Cleaning

How well can the analyst deal with malformed or inconsistent data?
  • Analyst can handle variable recoding.
  • Analyst can transform variables to different types, e.g. continuous to discrete.
  • Analyst can normalize a column of different types to a single type, e.g. numbers stored as [4, 'Four', '4.0', 4.0]
  • Analyst applies regularization where appropriate, and documents why / associated tests.
  • Analyst writes code to deal with specific instances / errors.
  • Analyst writes code to validate data format and generate statistics on types of errors.
  • Analyst can process semi-structured data, e.g. text or HTML.
  • Analyst can handle data collected from systems with poor validation, such as mispellings or different formats. e.g. ["Adam", "Aadm"] and ["1/2/2001", "1.2.2001", "2001-2-1", "January 2, 2001", "2nd Jan 2001"]
  • Analyst writes code to solve the general problem, and code works on new data. (Note: this can be risky if your solution is wrong. Data corruption, not cleaning!)

Data Storage & Organization

How does the employee store the data? Do they handle common issues such as updates and change tracking?
  • Analyst stores data in flat files on their computer.
  • Analyst performs difference checks on updated data, and stores a record of the changes.
  • Analyst organizes data (input) and data (processed) separately.
  • Analyst employs a logical, documented naming scheme.
  • Analyst keeps a log of all data updates.
  • Analyst ensures that all data (especially updates) are stored in a consistent format.
  • Analyst stores data in an appropriate database, either SQL or non-SQL.
  • Analyst creates logical, related database schema.
  • Analyst creates extensive documentation on storage/organization choices.
  • Bonus: Analyst stores data in centralized system.
  • Bonus: Analyst creates generalized storage system that can be re-used across projects.

Data Sharing

How well does the employee make data available to other team members?
  • Employee provides data when a team member requests it.
  • Employee provides data in the requested format.
  • Employee compiles and publishes list of data available.
  • Employee actively monitors what data team members use, how, and provide data where appropriate.
  • Employee provides detailed documentation of data along with data.
  • Employee creates API endpoint for people to query data directly (example tool).
  • Employee documents API and provides example usage.
  • Employee provides interactive dashboard to query data (example tool).

Delivery

What is the output of an analysis? How are the results delivered?
  • Analyst deliver results in requested format.
  • Analyst asks questions to identify how the result will be used, and proposes best format (for instance, certain graphs – may need to match arbitrary visual style).
  • Analyst provides link to "living document" on web server (in addition to other formats).

Repeatable Research
  • Analyst needs to manually perform each step. Research is not repeatable, even with the same data.
  • Research can be easily repeated by e.g. re-running a script or a Rdoc.
  • Research can be scheduled to run on a time interval.
  • New data can be run through the same analysis without modification.

Industry Practice

Does the analyst know how other people working on the same types of problems solve them?
  • Analyst can recognize different types of problems.
  • Analyst can find educational materials to gain further knowledge on trade-offs.
  • Analyst knows what commercial and academic organizations are working on.
  • Analyst has contacts who can provide guidance or details on how to solve different problems.
  • Analyst attends industry conferences and remains up to date.
  • Analyst contributes to cutting edge in the field.
  • Analyst frequently presents at conferences and industry events.

Organization

How well does the analyst organize their materials - data files, associated analyses, etc. Are related files placed together? Are all project materials easy to find? How do they handle version control?
  • Analyst groups files together according to personal preference.
  • Analyst organizes files on a per-project basis.
  • Analyst provides documentation (e.g. in a README) about project background, requirements, goals, and next steps.
  • Documentation includes description of files and folder structure.
  • Analyst employs Git or other VCS for version control.
  • Analyst provides detailed commit messages.
  • Analyst pushes to VCS server.

Interpretation

How well does the analyst interpret results for the business?
  • Analyst provides output of statistical process.
  • Analyst describes mangitude of effect and significance.
  • Analyst explains the impact the result has on the business issue/question.
  • Analyst provides detailed interpretation that can be used internally (by the team performing the investigation).
  • Analyst explains how the result may / may not affect related business issues separate from the one under investigation.
  • Analyst provides interpretation that can be used directly (e.g. forwarded to upper management or client).
  • Analyst correctly chooses the level of detail and means of presentation, e.g. file format + tables/graphs/etc.

Research Programs

How well can the analyst formulate a series of related experiments to answer a larger question?
  • Analyst performs analysis on an ad-hoc basis.
  • Analyst uses previous work to inform current or future work.
  • Analyst produces work in anticipation of future use (e.g. organization / creation – for instance, writing functions / packages).
  • Analyst designs series of studies (path dependent) to broader business issues.
  • Analyst explains how work performed in the past can be leveraged to positive impact.

Bayesian/Frequentist

How well does the analyst understand different approaches to statistics, and which is more appropriate in different situations?
  • Analyst is aware of difference.
  • Analyst can explain the difference in approach and the implications on analysis and interpretation.
  • Analyst advocates for one approach over another.
  • Analyst chooses between approaches based on the question at hand.

  • Employee can perform appropriate statistical tests.
  • Employee can verify underlying assumptions.

  • Employee will propose experiments when possible (even if larger team does not request them).
  • Employee delivers comprehensive overview of potential causal relationships.
  • Employee may use graphical models ala Judea Pearl.

Big Data

How well can the analyst work with large scale data?
  • Employee is limited to local workstation.
  • Employee can SSH into remote server for larger datasets (e.g. server with 244 GB RAM)
  • Employee can write distributed processing jobs in Hadoop MapReduce or Spark.
  • Employee writes jobs to minimize unnecessary network traffic and disk IO.
  • Employee can setup, operate, and teardown a cluster.
